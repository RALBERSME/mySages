Thales attempted to explain the world not through myths, but through logical thinking and the observation of nature.
He was one of the first to research the principles (archē) in nature.



Thales is often called the first Greek philosopher because he is said to have attempted to explain the world through natural principles rather than interpreting it primarily through myth. His supposed contributions include:

• Natural philosophy and explanation of nature: Thales searched for the primal principle of the world. He is said to have considered water the arche that underlies all things, an interpretation that is understood today as a symbolic statement about an underlying, all-encompassing principle. Whether he actually considered water to be the source of all things or whether this is a highly interpretive attribution is disputed; what is certain, however, is that he claimed to be seeking natural causes.

• Geometry and Mathematics: Thales is associated with early geometric theorems, including the famous theorem that a circle is uniquely determined by three points and that a circumcircle of a triangle divides its diameter from its center. The Thales Selo (theorem)—that an angle in a semicircle is a right angle—is often attributed to or associated with him, although historical evidence for it is fragmentary. Nevertheless, he is considered a pioneer of geometry, using mathematics as a tool for knowledge.

• Astronomy and calendars: He is credited with observations that distinguish him: systematizing cosmic phenomena, such as those that serve as a precursor to scientific astronomy. History tells us that he had similar ideas about moonlight and the position of the sun, making him an early observer of the sky.

• Technology and Finance: Thales is said to have used a clever method of capacity and observation to determine the value of oil – the legend of Thales, who predicted oil prices and thus demonstrated an economic form of forecasting by relying on predictions and observations. Whether this story actually happened historically remains unclear, but it stands as a symbolic story that knowledge can also be used in practical matters. Why he is counted among the Seven Sages The Seven Sages – the Seven Wise Men of Greece – are a group of lawmakers, poets, and wise strategists who supposedly lived in the 7th to 6th centuries BC. The list of the Seven Sages varies depending on the source; in popular tradition, Thales, Solon, Bias, Pittacus, Cleobulus, Periander, and Chilon are often mentioned.

• Teaching and influence: Thales is said to have had many students and thus spread the idea of ​​understanding the world through rational observation. His approach laid the foundation for later Greek natural philosophy.

• Myth vs. Science: The legends surrounding Thales demonstrate the transition from mythological explanations to scientific explanations – a sign of the change in ancient scientific culture. • Art and Architecture: Through geometric thinking, Thales also influenced applications in architecture and urban planning, where precise measurements were important.

• Legacy: Thales serves as a symbol of the fact that curiosity, powers of observation, and logical thinking are the foundations of all science.

• Because of his role as one of the first to seek a rational explanation for natural phenomena, and because of his contributions to geometry and mathematical thought, Thales is considered one of the Seven Sages.
